The Science

What the Lab Actually Does

Every cell in your body has 46 chromosomes -- 23 pairs. Cancer cells almost always have the wrong number: extra copies of some, missing copies of others. This is called aneuploidy, and it's one of the defining features of cancer. Uri Ben-David's lab studies how to turn this abnormality into a weapon against the disease.

1

Discover the Pattern

Find which chromosomal changes appear in specific cancer types. Is chromosome 8 always gained in this tumor? Is chromosome 10 always lost? Map the landscape of chromosomal chaos.

2

Understand the Mechanism

Figure out why cancer cells keep these changes. Do extra copies of a chromosome give the tumor an advantage? Do they carry genes the cancer depends on for survival?

3

Exploit the Vulnerability

If cancer cells depend on having extra chromosomes, then targeting those extras could kill the cancer while leaving normal cells (with the right number) unharmed. This is the therapeutic opportunity.

W

Wet Lab (Experimental)

Growing cancer cell lines, working with mouse models, obtaining patient tumor samples from hospitals, running microscopy, performing DNA/RNA sequencing. Hands-on work with biological materials.

D

Dry Lab (Computational)

Analyzing sequencing data with bioinformatics pipelines, statistical modeling, machine learning, mining public genomic databases. Turning raw data into scientific insight.

T

Translation to Clinic

Several lab discoveries are already being tested in clinical settings: improved diagnostics, new drug targets, and better understanding of how tumors resist treatment.

The Data

How Data Flows Through the Lab

The lab generates and consumes multiple types of data from multiple sources. Understanding this flow is the first step to organizing it.

The Research Data Pipeline

Data Sources

Open-source databases (public genomic repositories like GEO, TCGA) -- always re-downloadable.

Collaboration data from hospitals and other labs -- under NDA, must be stored securely.

Self-generated from lab experiments -- sequencing sent to external facility, returns as FASTQ files.

→

Processing

Sequencing: Raw FASTQ files are aligned to a reference genome, quality-checked, then analyzed (copy number calls, gene expression, variant detection).

Imaging: Microscopy images (~500GB/run) are annotated and quantified -- currently by hand for some instruments.

→

Analysis & Output

Statistical analysis, visualization, interpretation. Results go into paper drafts.

On publication: data deposited to public repository (gets a unique ID). Pre-publication data has no standard backup strategy.

What Sequencing Data Looks Like

For non-biologists: sequencing data arrives as FASTQ files. Each DNA/RNA fragment is stored as 4 lines of text -- an identifier, the actual sequence (letters A, T, C, G), a separator, and quality scores. A single sample can produce millions of fragments, resulting in files of several gigabytes.

Line 1 (ID):     @SEQ_ID_SAMPLE_001/1
Line 2 (Sequence): GATTTGGGGTTCAAAGCAGTATCGATCAAA
Line 3 (Separator): +
Line 4 (Quality):  !''*((((***+))%%%++)(%%%%).1***

The Missing Piece

There is no catalog of what data exists in the lab, where it is, who generated it, what state it's in (raw / processed / published / archived), or whether it's backed up. Nobody has the full picture -- not even Uri, the lab director.

Where Things Break

Ten Problems We Found

Prioritized by impact. Red = high impact, orange = medium, gray = structural (won't go away but can be managed).

1

Haia is the computational bottleneck

Non-computational students depend on a single bioinformatician for every analysis request. When she's busy, research stalls.

"Haia, run this for me" -- the default request from most experimentalists
2

No data catalog or inventory

Nobody knows what data the lab has collectively. Datasets are discoverable only if you know who generated them and can track them down personally.

"We don't have the macro view" -- Uri Ben-David
3

Knowledge loss when students leave

Students work 3-5 years then graduate. Their scripts, data organization, analysis decisions, and institutional knowledge leave with them. The next student starts from scratch.

"The next student who picks up the project starts from scratch -- different environment, different folder structure, incomplete docs"
4

Duplicated reference data

Every computational student downloads and stores their own copy of reference genomes (~50GB each). Five students = 250GB of identical data on the same server.

5

No standardized compute environment

Each student installs their own packages, their own Python/R versions, their own tools. A new student spends roughly a week just getting set up.

6

Scattered storage with no index

Data lives across 6+ locations. There's no way to search "do we have sequencing data from breast cancer cell lines?" without asking every person in the lab.

7

No validation of ad-hoc analyses

Experimentalists use ChatGPT-generated R scripts without understanding or verifying them. Statistical errors could propagate into published research.

"They don't even know how to validate what ChatGPT gives them" -- Haia Khoury
8

Communication across 5+ channels

Lab members communicate via Slack, WhatsApp, university email, personal email, and Google Docs. Finding a past conversation means searching everywhere.

Haia created a separate Gmail just to aggregate messages from all channels into one searchable place
9

Academic budget constraints

Unlike a tech company, the lab can't throw money at cloud solutions. Every tool must be free, open-source, or use existing university infrastructure.

10

Resistance to change

Biologists are conservative. Slack adoption took months of persistent pushing. Some students still refuse. Any solution must be obviously easier, not just theoretically better.

The Future

What AI Makes Possible

The real payoff isn't just organization -- it's intelligence. Once lab data is structured and standardized, AI can extract insights that no individual researcher could find alone.

🔍

Cross-Project Pattern Finder

"Student A's cell line shows the same copy number pattern as the patient data Student B is analyzing." Connections that span years and projects, surfaced automatically.

✅

Automated QC Validator

An AI reviewer that checks non-computational students' analyses for common errors: wrong statistical test, insufficient sample size, multiple comparison issues -- before they reach a paper draft.

💬

Natural Language Data Query

"Show me all sequencing data from breast cancer cell lines processed in the last year." Query the entire lab's data catalog in plain language -- no SQL, no folder hunting.

📚

Literature Linker

Automatically connect lab findings to relevant published papers. Surface new publications that relate to ongoing projects. Keep every researcher in the loop without manual literature reviews.

"The potential here is dramatic. If we can create an effective system for managing this data, there's enormous knowledge that could be unlocked. If we don't, that knowledge stays on the floor."

-- Yair Uziel, Tech for Cancer

The Strategy

Why Small Steps Matter

The biggest risk to this project isn't technical -- it's adoption. Biologists are conservative, each student feels ownership of their workflow, and past standardization attempts have seen resistance. Our approach: don't announce a new system -- make the new way obviously better and let people discover it.

1

Start With Champions

Haia and Yael are already pushing for improvement. We amplify their efforts, not compete with them. They co-design every solution.

2

Win the Early Adopters

Hybrid researchers are tech-curious and open to change. When they adopt standardized tools, they influence experimentalists by example.

3

Build for Experimentalists

Give non-coders tools so simple that using them is easier than the current ad-hoc approach. Adoption should feel like a shortcut, not a mandate.

4

Computational Last

Independent coders are hardest to standardize. Let them see the infrastructure benefits (shared environments, no duplication, faster onboarding) before asking them to change.

Reference

Glossary for Non-Biologists

Key terms from the lab's world, translated to plain English.

Aneuploidy
Having the wrong number of chromosomes in a cell. Cancer cells almost always have this.
FASTQ
A text file format for storing DNA/RNA sequencing data. Each "read" is 4 lines of text.
Copy Number
How many copies of a gene or chromosome segment a cell has. Normal = 2. Cancer cells often have more or fewer.
Pipeline
A sequence of computational steps that process data automatically -- like an assembly line for data analysis.
Reference Genome
The "standard" human genome that researchers compare their sequencing data against. About 3 billion letters.
Cell Line
Cancer cells grown in the lab that can be kept alive indefinitely for experiments. Some are decades old.
PI (Principal Investigator)
The professor who leads the lab. Responsible for funding, direction, and scientific output. Uri Ben-David in this case.
Wet Lab / Bench
Physical lab work with actual biological materials -- cells, chemicals, microscopes, pipettes.
Dry Lab / Computational
Computer-based analysis of biological data. No test tubes -- just code and data.
Sequencing
Reading the DNA or RNA code of a biological sample, letter by letter (A, T, C, G).
Bioinformatics
Using computer science to analyze biological data. The bridge between biology and data science.
QC (Quality Control)
Checks to ensure data and results meet quality standards before being used for conclusions.
